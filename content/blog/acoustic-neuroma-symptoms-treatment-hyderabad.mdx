Acoustic Neuroma, also known as **Vestibular Schwannoma**, is a rare, non-cancerous (benign) tumor that develops on the main nerve leading from your inner ear to your brain. This nerve, known as the vestibulocochlear nerve (eighth cranial nerve), branches directly influences your balance and hearing. Pressure from an acoustic neuroma can cause hearing loss, ringing in your ear, and unsteadiness.

While the word "tumor" can be frightening, it is important to understand that acoustic neuromas are slow-growing and do not spread to other parts of the body. However, as they grow, they can press against the brainstem and vital structures, making timely diagnosis and expert management essential.

## Common Symptoms: What to Look Out For

The symptoms of an acoustic neuroma often develop subtly and gradually over years. Because they can be easily mistaken for aging or other ear problems, diagnosis is sometimes delayed.

The most common early signs include:

*   **Unilateral Hearing Loss:** Gradual, progressive loss of hearing in **only one ear** is the hallmark symptom. In rare cases, hearing loss can be sudden.
*   **Tinnitus:** A ringing, buzzing, or hissing sound in the affected ear.
*   **Unsteadiness or Balance Issues:** You might feel off-balance or have difficulty walking in a straight line.
*   **Dizziness or Vertigo:** While true spinning vertigo is less common, a general sense of dizziness is frequent.
*   **Facial Numbness:** As the tumor grows, it can press on the adjacent trigeminal nerve, causing numbness or tingling on one side of the face.

> **Note:** If you experience persistent hearing loss in one ear or unexplained tinnitus, it is crucial to consult a specialist for a thorough evaluation. Early detection significantly improves treatment outcomes.

## Diagnosing Acoustic Neuroma

If an acoustic neuroma is suspected, a combination of hearing tests and advanced imaging is used to confirm the diagnosis.

### 1. Audiometry (Hearing Test)
A comprehensive hearing test is the first step. It detects "sensorineural hearing loss," which indicates damage to the nerve or inner ear. The test will check your ability to hear sounds at different frequencies and your ability to distinguish speech.

### 2. MRI with Contrast (Gadolinium)
The gold standard for diagnosing acoustic neuroma is a **Contrast-Enhanced MRI of the Brain**. This scan provides detailed images of the internal auditory canal and cerebellopontine angle, allowing neurosurgeons to see even very small tumors (as small as 1-2 mm).

For patients who cannot undergo MRI, a CT scan may be used, though it is less sensitive for small tumors.

## Treatment Options in Hyderabad

Treatment for acoustic neuroma depends on the size of the tumor, its growth rate, your age, and your overall health. At our clinic in Hyderabad, we adopt a personalized approach, discussing all viable options with you.

### 1. Observation ("Watch and Wait")
For small tumors that are not growing or causing significant symptoms, especially in older patients, immediate intervention may not be necessary. We monitor the tumor with regular MRI scans (typically every 6-12 months). If the tumor remains stable, no treatment is needed.

### 2. Microsurgery
Surgical removal is often recommended for:
*   Large tumors pressing on the brainstem.
*   Tumors that are growing rapidly.
*   Younger patients where long-term control is prioritized.

**Microsurgery** involves removing the tumor through a precise opening in the skull. The goal is to remove as much of the tumor as possible while preserving the facial nerve (which controls facial movement) and, if possible, hearing.

We utilize advanced techniques such as **Intraoperative Neuromonitoring** to constantly safeguard the facial and hearing nerves during the procedure.

### 3. Stereotactic Radiosurgery (Gamma Knife / CyberKnife)
Radiosurgery is a non-invasive alternative to open surgery. It delivers a highly focused dose of radiation to the tumor, stopping its growth and causing it to shrink over time.

It is often a preferred option for:
*   Small to medium-sized tumors (less than 3 cm).
*   Patients who are not ideal candidates for open surgery.
*   Recurrent tumors or tumor remnants after surgery.

## Recovery and Rehabilitation

Recovery after acoustic neuroma treatment varies based on the approach used.

*   **After Surgery:** Most patients spend 3-5 days in the hospital. You may experience temporary dizziness or headache, which improves with time. Facial weakness, if present, is managed with physiotherapy.
*   **After Radiosurgery:** This is typically a day-care procedure or requires a short hospital stay. Recovery is rapid, with most patients returning to normal activities within a few days.

**Vestibular Rehabilitation:** For patients experiencing balance issues, specific exercises help the brain compensate and improve stability.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is an acoustic neuroma cancer?
No, it is a benign (non-cancerous) tumor. It does not spread to other parts of the body, but it can cause problems by growing and pressing on nearby nerves and the brainstem.

### Will I lose my hearing completely after surgery?
Hearing preservation depends on the tumor's size and your pre-operative hearing level. For small tumors, hearing preservation is possible. For larger tumors, hearing in the affected ear may be lost, but options like BAHA (Bone Anchored Hearing Aids) or CROS hearing aids can help.

### How long does the surgery take?
Acoustic neuroma surgery is complex and typically takes 4 to 8 hours, depending on the tumor size and difficulty.

### Can the tumor come back?
Recurrence is rare after complete surgical removal. With radiosurgery, the goal is control rather than removal, and long-term success rates are very high (over 90%).
