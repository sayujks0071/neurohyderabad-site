What is Sciatica?

Sciatica is not a condition itself, but a symptom of an underlying problem involving the sciatic nerve. This nerve is the longest and thickest in the body, running from your lower back, through your hips and buttocks, and down each leg.

The pain typically affects one side of the body and can range from a mild ache to a sharp, burning sensation or excruciating pain. It may feel like a jolt or electric shock.

Dr. Sayuj Krishnan specializes in identifying the root cause of your sciatica to provide targeted relief, whether through medication, therapy, or advanced endoscopic procedures.

Common Symptoms

  • Pain radiating from lower back to buttock and down the leg
  • Numbness, tingling, or muscle weakness in the affected leg
  • Sharp pain that may make it difficult to stand up or walk
  • Pain that worsens when sitting, coughing, or sneezing
  • Current-like sensation shooting down the leg

Causes & Risk Factors

Herniated Disc

The most common cause. A slipped disc presses on the nerve root in the lower back.

Spinal Stenosis

Narrowing of the spinal canal reduces space for the nerve, often due to aging.

Spondylolisthesis

One vertebra slips forward over another, pinching the nerve.

Piriformis Syndrome

The piriformis muscle in the buttocks spasms and irritates the sciatic nerve.

Bone Spurs

Overgrowth of bone on the vertebrae can compress the nerves.

Trauma or Injury

Accidents or falls can injure the lumbar spine or nerve roots.

Red Flags: When to Seek Immediate Care

Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:

  • Sudden, severe pain in your low back or leg
  • Numbness or muscle weakness in your leg
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control (Cauda Equina Syndrome)
  • Pain following a violent injury, such as a traffic accident

Diagnosis & Treatment Approach

How We Diagnose Sciatica

Accurate diagnosis is key to effective treatment. Dr. Sayuj Krishnan uses a combination of:

  • 1. Physical ExaminationChecking muscle strength, reflexes, and performing the straight leg raise test to reproduce pain.
  • 2. Medical HistoryReviewing your symptoms, pain patterns, and any past injuries or conditions.
  • 3. Advanced ImagingMRI scans are the gold standard to visualize nerve compression. CT scans or X-rays may also be used.

Comprehensive Treatment Options

üåø Conservative Treatment

Most cases resolve with non-surgical care within 4-6 weeks.

  • Medication ManagementAnti-inflammatories, muscle relaxants, and neuropathic pain medications to reduce symptoms.
  • Physical TherapyTargeted exercises to improve posture, strengthen back muscles, and improve flexibility.
  • Pain InterventionsEpidural steroid injections or nerve blocks to reduce inflammation around the irritated nerve.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the fastest way to cure sciatica?‚ñº

There is no instant cure, but relief can be accelerated. Rest for 1-2 days (not more), use ice/heat packs, take prescribed anti-inflammatories, and perform gentle stretching. If pain persists, consult a specialist for targeted injections or therapy.

Can sciatica go away on its own?‚ñº

Yes, mild sciatica often resolves within 4-6 weeks with self-care. However, if the pain is severe, causes weakness, or lasts longer than 6 weeks, medical intervention is necessary to prevent permanent nerve damage.

Is walking good for sciatica?‚ñº

Yes, walking is generally good for sciatica as it improves blood flow and reduces inflammation. However, listen to your body‚Äîtake short walks and stop if the pain worsens. Avoid high-impact activities.

How do I know if I need surgery for sciatica?‚ñº

Surgery is typically considered if: 1) You have significant muscle weakness or loss of bowel/bladder control (emergency), 2) Pain is unbearable despite conservative treatment, or 3) Symptoms persist for more than 6-12 weeks and affect your quality of life.

What is the success rate of endoscopic spine surgery for sciatica?‚ñº

Endoscopic discectomy has a high success rate (over 90%) for relieving leg pain caused by disc herniation. It offers faster recovery and less pain compared to open surgery.
